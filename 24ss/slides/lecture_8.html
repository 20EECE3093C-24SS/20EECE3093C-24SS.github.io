Requirements Engineering

What is Requirements Engineering?

Requirements engineering is the process of discovering, documenting and maintaining a set of requirements for a computer-based system. The term engineering is choiceful, as it implies a systematic approach to the process of requirements gathering, analysis, management, etc.

What are Requirements?

Requirements are a description of what a system should do, and ultimately what to build. Importantly, they are not a description of how the system should do it.

  • They describe the problem (or need), not the solution.
  • The tell us what the system should do, not how it should do it.
  • They reflect the system design, but not the implementation (e.g., the software design)

Elementary Tasks

Elementary tasks encompass a variety of activities, such as:

  • Eliciting requirements involves identifying all relevant requirements.
  • Analyzing requirements consists of understanding requirements and achieving consensus.
  • Specifying requirements entails structuring, modeling, and documenting requirements.
  • Validating and verifying requirements ensures their validity and quality.

Requirements Elicitation

Introduction

Requirements elicitation involves identifying and obtaining requirements from the primary sources for a new system. The key sources of these requirements are stakeholders, who may have various levels of interest and involvement in the system.

Emphasize the importance of accurately identifying stakeholders and understanding their needs as a foundational step in the development process. Discuss strategies for engaging stakeholders to ensure their requirements are comprehensively captured.

Challenges in Requirements Elicitation

Elicitation is critical for defining project requirements but comes with its unique set of challenges. Recognizing these challenges is vital for a successful elicitation process.

Key Challenges

  • Distributed knowledge among sources
  • Difficulties in articulating tacit knowledge
  • Limited observability of current system issues
  • Bias from stakeholders

Highlight the importance of awareness and preparation in overcoming the challenges of requirements elicitation. Discuss examples of common challenges and their impact on the elicitation process.

Distributed Knowledge: Bridging Gaps

Knowledge about system requirements is often dispersed across various sources and not readily available in explicit forms. Conflicting information from different sources necessitates a strategic approach to harmonize insights.

Stress the need for comprehensive strategies to gather and consolidate distributed knowledge. Explore methods for identifying and reconciling conflicting requirements to achieve a cohesive understanding.

Tacit Knowledge and the “Say-Do” Gap

Capturing tacit knowledge—what people know but do not articulate—is a significant challenge. The discrepancy between what stakeholders say and their actual practices can complicate requirements elicitation.

Discuss techniques for uncovering tacit knowledge, such as observation and indirect questioning, and the importance of recognizing the say-do gap in elicitation efforts.

Overcoming Limited Observability

The natural behavior of system users may be altered by the observer’s presence, known as the Probe Effect or Hawthorne Effect. Strategies must be developed to minimize these effects for genuine insights.

Delve into the implications of the Probe and Hawthorne Effects on data accuracy. Discuss observational techniques that can help mitigate these effects and ensure the reliability of gathered information.

Addressing Stakeholder Bias

Stakeholder biases can influence the elicitation process, with some stakeholders hesitant to share crucial information. Implementing strategies to recognize and mitigate these biases is essential for unbiased requirements gathering.

Explore methods to identify and address stakeholder biases, such as anonymous surveys or facilitated workshops, to ensure a balanced and equitable elicitation process.

Strategies

Best Practices

  • Engage with Users: Collaborate closely with users to understand their workflow and challenges.
  • Continuous Inquiry: Actively ask questions to uncover deep insights and hidden requirements.
  • Understand User Intent: Focus on understanding the reasons behind user interactions, not just the actions themselves.
  • Embrace Flexibility: Be prepared for requirements to evolve as understanding deepens and project conditions change.

Common Pitfalls

  • Avoid Over-Specification: Keep descriptions clear and concise without delving into excessive detail.
  • Steer Clear of Complex Logic: Refrain from outlining intricate business logic or detailed system rules prematurely.
  • Don’t Predefine UI Details: Avoid specifying the precise user interface design early in the process.
  • Resist the Urge to Plan Everything: Acknowledge that it’s impossible to anticipate every scenario or need.
  • Eliminate Unnecessary Features: Do not incorporate features that do not have a clear demand from customers.

Additional Considerations

Feature Creep and Its Impacts

What is Feature Creep? - The incremental addition of features to a product beyond its original scope and necessity, often leading to complexity and bloat.

Causes of Feature Creep: - Developers’ enthusiasm for coding new features. - Sales teams’ desire to enhance product appeal. - Users’ perceived need for more functionality.

Impacts of Feature Creep: - Risk to Timely Delivery: Unplanned features delay product launches. - Quality and Usability Issues: Increases in options lead to more bugs, delays, and decreased testing coverage. - The “Boiled Frog” Syndrome: Gradual yet significant negative impact on the product, unnoticed until it’s too late.

Mitigation Strategies: - Prioritize essential features based on clear, strategic objectives. - Implement a robust change management process. - Educate stakeholders on the importance of focus and discipline in product development.

Types of Requirements

Introduction to Types of Requirements

Requirements are fundamental in guiding the development and evaluation of projects, systems, or products. They vary widely, encompassing more than just system functionalities, to include business needs, user expectations, and operational constraints.

Start with an overview of the broad spectrum of requirements types, emphasizing their role in ensuring successful project outcomes and stakeholder satisfaction.

Types of Requirements Explained

Business Requirements

Define the high-level objectives and goals of an organization that a project or product aims to fulfill. They are strategic in nature and focus on the benefits to the business.

User Requirements

Describe what end-users expect from a system in terms of functionality and usability. They are typically captured through user stories or use cases.

System Requirements

Split into functional and non-functional requirements, detailing what the system should do and how it should perform, respectively.

Software Requirements

Specific to software systems, detailing functionalities, interface designs, and performance metrics required for the software.

Hardware Requirements

Specify the physical and technical specifications needed to support the system, including processors, memory, and storage.

Dive into each type of requirement, explaining their focus, importance, and how they contribute to the comprehensive planning and execution of projects.

Beyond System Requirements

Regulatory Requirements

Mandate compliance with laws, regulations, and standards relevant to the project or product. Essential for legal and ethical operations.

Data Requirements

Detail the specifics of data management, including data collection, storage, manipulation, and privacy considerations.

Security Requirements

Define the measures needed to protect the system from unauthorized access, data breaches, and other security threats.

Quality Requirements

Set the standards for the overall quality of the project or product, including reliability, maintainability, and scalability.

Expand on the requirements that go beyond the system’s immediate functionalities to include broader operational, legal, and quality aspects. Highlight the necessity of each for comprehensive project planning.

Documenting and Managing Requirements

Effective requirements management involves the documentation, verification, and validation of all types of requirements. This ensures alignment with project goals and stakeholder expectations throughout the development process.

Conclude with best practices for managing the diverse types of requirements, including the use of requirements management tools, regular stakeholder engagement, and iterative review processes.

Conclusion: The Importance of Comprehensive Requirements Analysis

A thorough analysis of all types of requirements is crucial for the success of any project. It ensures that the final product is not only technically sound but also aligns with business objectives, meets user expectations, complies with regulatory standards, and achieves quality benchmarks.

Summarize the importance of a holistic approach to requirements analysis, touching on how it underpins project success and stakeholder satisfaction. Encourage ongoing learning and adaptation in requirements management practices.

System Requirements

Introduction to System Requirements

System requirements are categorized into functional and non-functional requirements. Together, they define what a system should do and how it should perform, serving as a blueprint for system development and evaluation.

Begin by introducing the distinction between functional and non-functional requirements. Emphasize their complementary roles in defining a comprehensive set of expectations for any system.

Understanding Functional Requirements

Functional requirements describe the specific behaviors or functions of a system. They detail the system’s operations, including data processing, user interactions, and other activities necessary to fulfill user needs.

Key Points

  • Define system operations
  • Focus on “what” the system should do
  • Based on user needs and business objectives

Dive deeper into functional requirements, explaining their focus on the system’s operations and their direct link to user needs and business goals. Highlight the importance of clarity and completeness.

Understanding Non-Functional Requirements

Non-functional requirements specify the system’s operational attributes and constraints, such as performance, security, usability, and reliability. They describe “how” the system performs its functions.

Key Points

  • Define system qualities
  • Focus on “how” the system performs
  • Essential for user satisfaction and system resilience
