Requirements Engineering

Requirements engineering is the process of discovering, documenting and maintaining a set of requirements for a computer-based system. The term engineering is choiceful, as it implies a systematic approach to the process of requirements gathering, analysis, management, etc.

What are Requirements?

Requirements are a description of what a system should do, and ultimately what to build. Importantly, they are not a description of how the system should do it.

  • They describe the problem (or need), not the solution.
  • The tell us what the system should do, not how it should do it.
  • They reflect the system design, but not the implementation (e.g., the software design)

Elementary Tasks

Elementary tasks encompass a variety of activities, such as:

  • Eliciting requirements involves identifying all relevant requirements.
  • Analyzing requirements consists of understanding requirements and achieving consensus.
  • Specifying requirements entails structuring, modeling, and documenting requirements.
  • Validating and verifying requirements ensures their validity and quality.

Requirements Elicitation

Requirements Elicitation

Requirements elicitation is the process of identifying the sources of requirements for a new system and obtaining those requirements from the sources. The main sources of requirements are stakeholders, who are individuals or organizations that have a direct or indirect interest in the system.

Challenges of Requirements Elicitation

  • Elicitation plays a crucial role in understanding project requirements.
  • Several inherent difficulties are encountered during the elicitation process.
  • Being aware of these challenges is essential for effective elicitation.

The Challenge of Distributed Knowledge

  • Knowledge is frequently scattered across multiple sources.
  • It is seldom available in an explicit or documented form.
  • Knowledge from different sources may conflict, highlighting the need for the principle of complementarity in conflict resolution.

Tacit Knowledge: The “Say-Do” Problem

  • Articulating knowledge used on a regular basis is often challenging for people.
  • Tacit knowledge, although critical, is difficult to capture in documentation.
  • The discrepancy between what people say and do complicates effective elicitation.

The Impact of Limited Observability

  • Owners of the problem may be too engaged with the current system to observe issues.
  • An observer’s presence might alter the natural behavior of subjects (Probe Effect, Hawthorne Effect).
  • It’s vital to recognize and account for these effects for accurate observation.

Navigating Bias

  • Stakeholders may hesitate or be unwilling to share essential information.
  • Their interest in influencing the project’s outcome can lead to bias.
  • Implementing awareness and mitigation strategies is crucial for reducing bias.

Ways to Overcome Challenges

  • Recognizing and preparing for these difficulties is the first step.
  • Utilizing a variety of elicitation techniques can help address these challenges.
  • Active listening and critical analysis are key in resolving conflicts between information sources.
  • Developing strategies to uncover tacit knowledge and counteract biases is essential.

Requirements Elicitation Techniques

The process of requirements elicitation employs a comprehensive array of techniques designed to gather insights and information from a variety of sources. These techniques not only help in understanding the needs and expectations of stakeholders but also ensure that the final product meets its intended goals and requirements.

  • Background Reading: Reviews existing documentation to understand the project domain, identifying key concepts and terminology.

  • Hard Data Collection: Gathers quantitative data using surveys, statistical analysis, and performance metrics to support decision-making.

  • Interviews: Engages stakeholders through structured conversations to gain insights into their needs, preferences, and experiences.

  • Questionnaires: Distributes questions to a wide audience to collect data on opinions, behaviors, and preferences.

  • Group Techniques: Employs workshops, brainstorming, and focus groups to harness group creativity and knowledge for identifying requirements.

  • Participant Observation: Observes users in their natural environment to gain contextual insights and identify unarticulated needs.

  • Ethnomethodology: Analyzes social interactions in their natural settings to understand cultural and social influences on requirements.

  • Knowledge Elicitation Techniques: Uses methods like protocol analysis and cognitive task analysis to extract knowledge from experts, aiding in understanding complex systems.

Background Reading

Overview: Background reading involves a comprehensive review of existing documentation related to the project’s domain. This includes analyzing documents, reports, and materials to build a foundational understanding of the subject.

Key Points: - Purpose: To identify key concepts, terminology, and the overall context of the project. - Sources: Industry reports, academic articles, company documentation, and regulatory standards. - Benefits: Provides a solid foundation for further elicitation efforts and helps avoid rework by leveraging existing knowledge.

Sources of information: company reports, organization charts, policy manuals, job descriptions, reports, documentation of existing systems, etc. Advantages: - Helps the analyst to get an understanding of the organization before meeting the people who work there. - Helps to prepare for other types of fact finding (e.g. by being aware of the business objectives of the organization). - may provide detailed requirements for the current system. Disadvantages: - written documents often do not match up to reality. - Can be long-winded with much irrelevant detail

Appropriate for - Whenever you not familiar with the organization being investigated.

Hard Data Collection and Sampling

This technique focuses on the collection of quantitative data through various methods to inform decision-making and requirement formulation.

Key Points: - Methods: Surveys, statistical analysis, performance metrics. - Purpose: To gather objective, measurable evidence that supports or refutes assumptions about the project. - Benefits: Offers a basis for decision-making grounded in quantifiable data.

Hard data includes facts and figures… - Forms, Invoices, financial information,… - Reports used for decision making,… - Survey results, marketing data,…

Sampling - Sampling used to select representative set from a population - Purposive Sampling - choose the parts you think are relevant without worrying about statistical issues - Simple Random Sampling - choose every kth element - Stratified Random Sampling - identify strata and sample each - Clustered Random Sampling - choose a representative subpopulation and sample it

Sample Size is important - balance between cost of data collection/analysis and required significance

Process: - Decide what data should be collected - e.g. banking transactions - Determine the population - e.g. all transactions at 5 branches over one week - Choose type of sample - e.g. simple random sampling - Choose sample size - e.g. every 20th transaction

Interviews

Overview: Interviews involve direct communication with stakeholders to gather detailed insights about their needs, preferences, and experiences.

Key Points: - Types: Structured (fixed questions), semi-structured (guideline questions with flexibility), and unstructured (open-ended conversation). - Participants: Users, customers, experts, and other stakeholders. - Benefits: Allows for the collection of nuanced information directly from the source, providing depth to the understanding of requirements.

Types: - Structured - agenda of fairly open questions - Open-ended - no pre-set agenda

Advantages - Rich collection of information - Good for uncovering opinions, feelings, goals, as well as hard facts - Can probe in depth, & adapt followup questions to what the person tells you

Disadvantages - Large amount of qualitative data can be hard to analyze - Hard to compare different respondents - Interviewing is a difficult skill to master

Watch for - Unanswerable questions (“how do you tie your shoelaces?”) - Tacit knowledge (and post-hoc rationalization) - Removal from context - Interviewer’s attitude may cause bias (e.g. variable attentiveness)

Questionnaires

Overview: Questionnaires are used to collect data from a broad audience about their opinions, behaviors, and preferences.

Key Points: - Design: Carefully structured questions designed to elicit clear, quantifiable responses. - Distribution: Online platforms, email, or paper-based, depending on the target audience. - Benefits: Efficient for reaching a large number of people and obtaining a wide array of data.

Advantages - Can quickly collect info from large numbers of people - Can be administered remotely - Can collect attitudes, beliefs, characteristics

Disadvantages - Simplistic (presupposed) categories provide very little context - No room for users to convey their real needs

Watch for: - Bias in sample selection - Bias in self-selecting respondents - Small sample size (lack of statistical significance) - Open ended questions (very hard to analyze!) - Leading questions (“have you stopped beating your wife?”) Appropriation (“What is this a picture of?”) - Ambiguous questions (I.e. not everyone is answering the same question)

Group Techniques

Overview: This approach leverages the collective knowledge and creativity of a group through collaborative sessions.

Key Points: - Methods: Workshops, brainstorming sessions, focus groups, Delphi method. - Purpose: To identify diverse requirements and solve complex problems through group consensus. - Benefits: Encourages the generation of a wide range of ideas and solutions, benefiting from diverse perspectives.

Types: - Focus Groups - Brainstorming

Advantages - More natural interaction between people than formal interview - Can gauge reaction to stimulus materials (e.g. mock-ups, storyboards, etc)

Disadvantages - May create unnatural groups (uncomfortable for participants) - Danger of Groupthink - May only provide superficial responses to technical questions - Requires a highly trained facilitator

Watch for - sample bias - dominance and submission

Participant Observation

Overview: Participant observation involves immersing in the user’s environment to observe interactions, activities, and challenges firsthand.

Key Points: - Approach: The observer either participates in the activities or watches as an outsider. - Objective: To gain deep contextual insights and uncover needs that are not immediately apparent. - Benefits: Provides a rich understanding of the user’s environment and the context of their requirements.

Advantages - Contextualized; - Reveals details that other methods cannot

Disadvantages - Extremely time consuming! - Resulting ‘rich picture’ is hard to analyze - Cannot say much about the results of proposed changes

Watch for - going native!

Ethnomethodology

Overview: A qualitative research technique focusing on the analysis of social interactions in natural settings to understand the social and cultural contexts influencing requirements.

Key Points: - Approach: Detailed observation and analysis of everyday activities and interactions. - Purpose: To uncover how social norms and cultural practices influence the formulation of requirements. - Benefits: Offers insights into the implicit assumptions and practices that shape system requirements.

 Ethnomethodology is a subarea of Anthropology Looks for behaviours that are culture-specific  E.g. Frenchmen brag about sexual conquests to gain status;  E.g. Americans brag about money to gain status.  Each of these topics is taboo in the other culture  Uses a very tightly controlled set of methods:  Conversational analysis  Measurement of body system functions - e.g. heartbeat  Non-verbal behaviour studies  Detailed video analysis These techniques are useful in capturing information about a social setting.  Other observation techniques can be applied: Time-motion study  who is where, when? Communication audit  who talks to whom about what? Use of tools - status symbols plus sharing rules

Knowledge Elicitation Techniques

Overview: These are specialized methods aimed at extracting both explicit and tacit knowledge from subject matter experts.

Key Points: - Methods: Protocol analysis, repertory grids, cognitive task analysis. - Objective: To capture expert knowledge that can inform the development process, particularly for complex systems and processes. - Benefits: Facilitates a deeper understanding of specialized domains, contributing to more accurate and comprehensive requirements.

Conclusion: Each elicitation technique offers unique advantages and can be tailored to the specific needs of a project. Combining multiple techniques often yields the best results, ensuring a well-rounded and comprehensive understanding of requirements.
