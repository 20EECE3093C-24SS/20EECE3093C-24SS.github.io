Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

SDLC Overview

The Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) is a structured methodology for designing, developing, and testing high-quality software.


It outlines a comprehensive, sequential process ensuring systematic progression and quality assurance from inception to completion.

System Development Life Cycle

The System Development Life Cycle (also SDLC) is a broader term encompassing not only software development but also hardware and other system components.


It involves creating and implementing a comprehensive, integrated system solution, including software applications, networks, and hardware infrastructure.

Other Lifecycles

  • Application Lifecycle Management (ALM)
    Manages the entire lifespan of an application from concept to retirement.
  • Product Development Life Cycle (PDLC)
    Involves the entire journey of a product from concept to market release and support.
  • Software Testing Life Cycle (STLC)
    Focuses on phases ensuring software quality from requirement analysis to test closure.

Other Lifecycles (continued)

  • Software Release Life Cycle (SRLC)
    Involves planning, development, testing, deployment, and maintenance of software.

Application Lifecycle Management (ALM) Manages an application’s lifespan from initial concept to retirement, encompassing design, development, deployment, maintenance, and end-of-life. Product Development Lifecycle (PDLC): Covers a product’s journey from concept to discontinuation. Involves market research, design, development, testing, launch, marketing, and support. Software Testing Life Cycle (STLC): A sequence of phases ensuring software quality, including requirement analysis, planning, test case development, environment setup, execution, and closure. Software Release Life Cycle (SRLC): Involves planning, developing, testing, deploying, maintaining, and reviewing software, guiding its release from inception to public availability.

SDLC Phases

The SDLC outlines a comprehensive, sequential process to ensure the systematic progression and quality assurance from inception to completion. The SDLC can be typically broken down into seven key stages:

  1. Project Planning
  2. Requirements Gathering & Analysis
  3. Design
  4. Coding or Implementation
  5. Testing

SDLC Phases (continued)

  1. Deployment
  2. Maintenance


Note

In practice, you may experience different interpretations of the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) stages. Different organizations or projects might present varying numbers and types of stages, reflecting the subjective and context-dependent nature of these processes.

Stage 1: Project Planning

Project Planning is the foundational stage of the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC). It sets the groundwork for the project by establishing its scope and objectives.

  • Key Activities: Defining the project’s scope, conducting feasibility studies, estimating costs, and gathering initial requirements.
  • Output: A project plan, which includes scope definition, a feasibility report, and initial cost estimations.

The first stage of SDLC is all about “What do we want?” Project planning is a vital role in the software delivery lifecycle since this is the part where the team estimates the cost and defines the requirements of the new software.

Stage 1: Project Planning Roles

  • Project Managers lead the planning stage, focusing on project scope, timeline, resources, and budget.
  • Project Sponsors and other business representatives are involved in defining goals and expectations.
  • Senior Development Team Members may also contribute to the planning, especially in technical aspects.

Stage 2: Requirements Gathering & Analysis

Requirements Gathering & Analysis involves in-depth discussions with stakeholders to capture the detailed requirements of the software product.

  • Key Activities: Detailed interaction with clients and stakeholders to gather comprehensive requirements, analysis of these requirements, and clarification of specifications.
  • Output: A detailed requirements document, which forms the basis for software design and development.

The second step of SDLC is gathering maximum information from the client requirements for the product. Discuss each detail and specification of the product with the customer. The development team will then analyze the requirements keeping the design and code of the software in mind. Further, investigating the validity and possibility of incorporating these requirements into the software system. The main goal of this stage is that everyone understands even the minute detail of the requirement. Hardware, operating systems, programming, and security are to name the few requirements.

Stage 2: Requirements Gathering & Analysis Roles

  • Business Analysts are key in gathering and analyzing requirements from customers, target market, and industry experts.
  • Project Managers may have a minor role in communication and managing timelines and scope changes.
  • Business Representatives provide detailed targets and insights about the business problem the software aims to solve.

Stage 3: Design

The Design phase is where the software’s conceptual blueprint is developed, based on the requirements gathered in the previous stage.

  • Key Activities: Creation of design specifications encompassing system architecture, database design, and interface designs. Involvement of stakeholders for feedback and approval.
  • Output: Complete design documentation, including system architecture and interface design specifications.

In the design phase (3rd step of SDLC), the program developer scrutinizes whether the prepared software suffices all the requirements of the end-user. Additionally, if the project is feasible for the customer technologically, practically, and financially. Once the developer decides on the best design approach, he then selects the program languages like Oracle, Java, etc., that will suit the software.

Once the design specification is prepared, all the stakeholders will review this plan and provide their feedback and suggestions. It is absolutely mandatory to collect and incorporate stakeholder’s input in the document, as a small mistake can lead to cost overrun.

Stage 3: Design Roles

  • Product Architects and Developers work on the design of the product, creating multiple design approaches.
  • UI/UX Designers focus on creating a visually interesting interface and ensuring a great user experience.
  • The design is often documented in a Design Document Specification (DDS).

Stage 4: Coding or Implementation

Coding or Implementation is the phase where the software design is translated into source code.

  • Key Activities: Writing and compiling code, module development, adherence to coding standards, and initial testing of individual components.
  • Output: Functional software modules and a fully assembled software system ready for detailed testing.

Time to code! It means translating the design to a computer-legible language. In this fourth stage of SDLC, the tasks are divided into modules or units and assigned to various developers. The developers will then start building the entire system by writing code using the programming languages they chose. This stage is considered to be one of the longest in SDLC. The developers need certain predefined coding guidelines, and programming tools like interpreters, compilers, debugger to implement the code.

The developers can show the work done to the business analysts in case if any modifications or enhancements required.

Stage 4: Coding or Implementation Roles

  • Developers translate the design into code, using various programming languages and tools.
  • Technical Leads communicate with Project Managers and Business Analysts, ensuring the development aligns with project goals.
  • Junior, Middle, and Senior Developers work together, each bringing different levels of expertise.

Stage 5: Testing

In the Testing phase, the developed software is extensively tested to ensure it meets all specified requirements.

  • Key Activities: Systematic testing for bugs, performance issues, and alignment with initial requirements, including unit, integration, and system testing.
  • Output: A thoroughly tested software product, documented test results, and a list of resolved defects.

Once the developers build the software, then it is deployed in the testing environment. Then the testing team tests the functionality of the entire system. In this fifth phase of SDLC, the testing is done to ensure that the entire application works according to the customer requirements.

After testing, the QA and testing team might find some bugs or defects and communicate the same with the developers. The development team then fixes the bugs and send it to QA for a re-test. This process goes on until the software is stable, bug-free and working according to the business requirements of that system.

Stage 5: Testing Roles

  • Quality Assurance (QA) Specialists test the software to identify bugs and issues.
  • Automation Engineers might be involved in automated testing.
  • The goal is to ensure the software meets the initial requirements and is free of defects.

Stage 6: Deployment

Deployment involves making the software available for use by end-users.

  • Key Activities: Deploying the software in the production environment, final user acceptance testing, and user training.
  • Output: The deployed software in its operational setting, along with supporting documentation and user manuals.

The sixth phase of SDLC: Once the testing is done, and the product is ready for deployment, it is released for customers to use. The size of the project determines the complexity of the deployment. The users are then provided with the training or documentation that will help them to operate the software. Again, a small round of testing is performed on production to ensure environmental issues or any impact of the new release.

Stage 6: Deployment Roles

  • Deployment Engineers handle the software’s release, making it available to customers. They have a very crucial role …

    1. Building the Deployment Package: They create the package that includes all the necessary components for installing, updating, or running the software. This process involves compiling code, including necessary libraries, and ensuring that the software is configured correctly for the deployment environment.

    2. Preparing the Deployment Environment: Deployment Engineers often prepare the environments where the software will be deployed, which can include tasks like setting up servers, configuring databases, and ensuring network connectivity.

    3. Automating Deployment Processes: They often work on automating the deployment process to make it as efficient and error-free as possible. This includes scripting the steps needed to deploy the software and setting up continuous integration/continuous deployment (CI/CD) pipelines.

    4. Testing the Deployment Process: Before the actual deployment, they might conduct test deployments to ensure everything works as expected. This can include load testing, verifying integration points, and ensuring that the software operates correctly in the target environment.

    5. Monitoring and Troubleshooting: Post-deployment, they monitor the software’s performance and quickly address any issues that arise. This can involve analyzing logs, fixing bugs, and making necessary adjustments to the deployment setup.

    6. Collaboration and Documentation: They often collaborate with other teams, such as development, testing, and operations, to ensure smooth deployment. They also document the deployment process and maintain records of the deployment details for future reference.

Stage 7: Maintenance

Maintenance is the ongoing process of updating the software and solving problems encountered in the live environment.

  • Key Activities: Ongoing software updates, bug fixing, adaptation to new requirements or environments, and user support.
  • Output: Regular software updates and patches, updated documentation, and ongoing user support.

The actual problem starts when the customer actually starts using the developed system and those needs to be solved from time to time. Maintenance is the seventh phase of SDLC where the developed product is taken care of. According to the changing user end environment or technology, the software is updated timely.

Stage 7: Maintenance Roles

  • Support Engineers and Maintenance Developers address ongoing maintenance, such as bug fixing and updates.
  • Help Desk or Technical Support Teams provide user support and collect feedback for further improvements.

Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) Methodologies

Overview of SDLC Methodologies

This overview provides an overview of various methodologies within the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC), including Traditional SDLC Models, Agile Methodologies, and DevOps. We will explore their distinct philosophies and strategies, highlighting how each approach systematically addresses the stages and challenges in software development.

SDLC Methodologies in Practice

As we have done, the SDLC is commonly envisioned as a sequence of seven key stages. It suggests a linear progression from one stage to the next.

However, in practice, not all methodologies adhere strictly to this sequence. Methodologies like Agile and DevOps, prevalent in contemporary software development, often take a more dynamic approach. They involve iterating or cycling through these stages in a more integrated and flexible manner, adapting to the specific needs of the project.

The SDLC serves as a versatile framework, accommodating various methodologies, from traditional sequential processes to more iterative and adaptable approaches.

Evolving SDLC Methodologies: From Linear to Dynamic (continued)

The shift from a linear to a more dynamic approach in the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) marks an important evolution in response to the rapidly changing field of software development. Factors driving this transition include:

  • Rapid Technological Advancements: Keeping pace with swift technological changes and emerging trends.
  • Complex Project Requirements: Navigating the growing intricacy of software projects, which demand ongoing innovation and sophisticated integrations.
  • Strengthened Risk Management and Quality Assurance: Enhancing the capacity for effective risk management and consistently delivering high-quality software.

Evolving SDLC Methodologies: From Linear to Dynamic (continued)


Important

Modern methodologies like Agile and DevOps emphasize flexibility, allowing for the revisiting and refining of different stages of development. This agility is crucial for addressing the unique challenges and requirements of each project in today’s fast-evolving technology landscape.

Traditional SDLC Models

Traditional SDLC models offer structured, step-by-step frameworks for managing the software development process, from initiation to deployment. These models are characterized by:

  • Sequential Progression: A defined sequence of phases in the development process.
  • Predictability and Documentation: Emphasis on detailed planning and extensive documentation.

Traditional SDLC Models (continued)

Key Traditional Models include:

  • Waterfall Model: Linear and sequential, ideal for projects with clear, well-defined requirements.
  • Iterative Model: Emphasizes repeated cycles of development for gradual improvement.
  • Incremental Model: Builds the system in increments, integrating components as developed.
  • V-Model (Verification and Validation): Enhances the Waterfall model with stringent testing at each phase.
  • Spiral Model: Merges iterative development with continuous risk analysis.
  • Rapid Application Development (RAD): Focuses on quick prototyping, minimizing planning.

Agile Methodologies

Agile methodologies, such as Scrum, XP (Extreme Programming), and Kanban, advocate a flexible and collaborative approach to software development. Their key characteristics include:

  • Iterative Development: Breaking down the project into small, manageable increments.
  • Flexibility and Customer Collaboration: Prioritizing adaptability and ongoing stakeholder engagement.
  • Frequent Deliveries: Regular release of functional software components for continuous improvement and adaptability.

DevOps

DevOps represents a cultural and practical shift, aiming to unify software development and operations. It is crucial in:

  • Collaborative Culture: Encouraging shared responsibilities and teamwork between development and operations.
  • Continuous Integration/Continuous Delivery (CI/CD): Automating the deployment process for improved efficiency.
  • Automated Testing and Monitoring: Implementing automated systems for more reliable and efficient software delivery.
