CI, CD, CD, DevOps

Continuous Integration (CI)

Continuous Integration (CI) is a development practice that requires developers to integrate code into a shared repository several times a day. Each check-in is then verified by an automated build, allowing teams to detect problems early. By integrating regularly, you can detect errors quickly, and locate them more easily.

Continuous Delivery (CD)

Continuous Delivery is an extension of Continuous Integration to make sure that you can release new changes to your customers quickly in a sustainable way. This means that on top of having automated your testing, you also have automated your release process and you can deploy your application at any point of time by clicking on a button.

Continuous Deployment (CD)

Continuous Deployment goes one step further than Continuous Delivery. With this practice, every change that passes all stages of your production pipeline is released to your customers. There’s no human intervention, and only a failed test will prevent a new change to be deployed to production.

CI/CD Pipeline

A CI/CD pipeline automates your software delivery process. The pipeline builds code, runs tests (CI), and safely deploys a new version of the application (CD). In a CI/CD pipeline, each step is automated and the pipeline is triggered by code changes.

  • The CI/CD pipeline automates the steps required to deliver your application to production.
  • The pipeline is triggered by code changes.
  • The pipeline builds code, runs tests, and safely deploys a new version of the application.
  • The key to a successful CI/CD pipeline is automation.

DevOps

DevOps is a set of practices that combines software development (Dev) and IT operations (Ops). It aims to shorten the systems development life cycle and provide continuous delivery with high software quality.

  • DevOps is complementary with Agile software development, it is a way of thinking and a way of working that enables individuals and organizations to develop and maintain products at a high velocity.
  • CI/CD development practices and DevOps philosophy share the mutual objective of accelerating and optimizing the software development process.

Software Engineering as a Discipline

Emergence as a Discipline

The historical development of software engineering as a recognized discipline:

  • 1940s: Invention of the first computers.
  • 1960s: Significant advancements in computing power and complexity.
  • 1965: Introduction of Moore’s Law by Gordon Moore, predicting the doubling of transistors on microchips approximately every two years.
  • Software Crisis: As computing capabilities expanded, programming techniques lagged, leading to inefficiencies and the so-called “software crisis”.
  • 1940s: The decade marked by the birth of computers, with ENIAC, one of the earliest computers, becoming operational in 1945. This period laid the foundational technologies that would drive future innovations.
  • 1960s: Computers such as the IBM System/360 emerged, which were vastly more powerful and capable of handling more complex calculations and processes than their predecessors.
  • 1965: Gordon Moore, co-founder of Intel, observed and formulated Moore’s Law, which became a critical predictor of the pace of semiconductor technology advancement and influenced strategic planning and research in the technology sector.
  • Software Crisis: Highlighted notably in the late 1960s, this crisis was recognized due to a growing discrepancy between the capabilities of fast-evolving hardware and the less swiftly advancing software, leading to widespread project failures, cost overruns, and software inefficiencies.

The Software Crisis

Key challenges identified during the software crisis:

  • Budget and Time Overruns: Projects consistently exceeded budgetary and time constraints.
  • Inefficiency and Low Quality: Widespread issues with inefficient operations and poor software quality.
  • Non-compliance with Requirements: Frequent inability to meet specified requirements.
  • Management and Maintenance Issues: Software projects were often unmanageable and the code difficult to maintain.
  • Budget and Time Overruns: Examples include the IBM System/360, which faced significant delays and budget issues due to its ambitious scope and technological challenges.
  • Inefficiency and Low Quality: Early software often lacked standardization, leading to issues such as the frequent system crashes and maintenance difficulties. For instance, early versions of the software for the Apollo missions required frequent debugging and reprogramming.
  • Non-compliance with Requirements: The lack of mature software development methodologies often resulted in software that did not meet user needs or was not user-friendly, complicating its deployment and utilization.
  • Management and Maintenance Issues: The absence of structured development processes led to ‘spaghetti code,’ which was difficult to manage, maintain, or upgrade.

NATO Software Engineering Conferences

The NATO Software Engineering Conference aimed to address the software crisis:

  • Sponsored by the NATO Science Committee in 1968 and 1969.
  • To promote the systematic, rigorous, and measurable development of software, ensuring projects were completed on time, within budget, and met specifications.
  • Embraced engineering principles that had been successful in other disciplines.
  • When and Where: The first conference was held in Garmisch, Germany, in 1968, drawing together leading computer scientists and engineers to discuss solutions to the software crisis.
  • Objective: The conferences aimed to transform ad hoc software creation into a disciplined engineering approach. Key discussions focused on adopting more formalized methodologies to enhance predictability and efficiency in software development.
  • Approach: Principles such as modular programming and structured design were advocated to improve software quality and maintainability. These principles were adapted from traditional engineering fields which had long used systematic approaches to complex projects.

Key Outcomes of the NATO Conferences

Major developments post-NATO conferences in software engineering:

  • Establishment of Formal Conferences: The 1st National Conference on Software Engineering in Washington DC, USA, held on September 11-12, 1975, was sponsored by the National Bureau of Standards and the IEEE Computer Society. It evolved into the 2nd International Conference on Software Engineering (ICSE).
  • Institutional Developments: Formation of the Software Engineering Institute (SEI) by IEEE Computer Society and ACM (Association for Computing Machinery) in 1984.
  • Establishment of Formal Conferences: The establishment of these conferences provided a regular forum for academia and industry to exchange knowledge, innovations, and best practices in software engineering. ICSE, in particular, has become one of the most influential gatherings in the field, promoting cutting-edge research and new methodologies.
  • Institutional Developments: The SEI, established at Carnegie Mellon University, has played a crucial role in advancing the discipline of software engineering. It has been instrumental in defining software engineering standards, promoting research, and developing educational curricula to train future software engineers.

Software Engineering Ethics

Background

The Code of Ethics for Software Engineers

  • Designed to address the ethical and professional challenges faced by software engineers by outlining their responsibilities to the public, clients, employers, and the profession as a whole.
  • Developed by the IEEE Computer Society (IEEE-CS) and the Association for Computing Machinery (ACM) Joint Task Force on Software Engineering Ethics and Professional Practices.
  • Officially adopted in 1999.
  • Background: Highlight the collaboration between IEEE-CS and ACM to establish ethical standards tailored to the unique challenges of software engineering.
  • Impact and Importance: Discuss how the Code guides software engineers in maintaining professional integrity and responsibility, thus influencing professional practices and ethical standards within the field.

Code of Ethics (Short Version)

Software engineers shall commit themselves to making the analysis, specification, design, development, testing and maintenance of software a beneficial and respected profession. In accordance with their commitment to the health, safety and welfare of the public, software engineers shall adhere to the following Eight Principles:

  1. Public - Act in the public interest.
  2. Client & Employer - Serve the best interests of clients and employers, compatible with the public good.
  3. Product: Ensure products meet the highest professional standards.
  4. Judgement: Maintain integrity and independence in professional judgment.
  5. Management: Promote an ethical approach to management of software development.
  6. Profession: Advance the integrity and reputation of the profession.
  7. Colleagues: Be fair and supportive of colleagues.
  8. Self: Participate in lifelong learning and ethical practice of the profession.
  • Purpose of the Short Version: Provides a concise overview of the Code, focusing on the key ethical commitments software engineers make.
  • Details and Applications: Each principle is an aspirational goal, setting standards for behavior that enhance professionalism and trust in software engineering.
  • Extended Guidance: The longer version of the Code includes detailed descriptions and scenarios to help engineers apply these principles in various professional contexts.

Principle 1: Public

Software engineers shall act consistently with the public interest.

  • Accept full responsibility for personal work outcomes.
  • Balance the interests of various stakeholders with the public good.
  • Approve software only if it is safe, meets specifications, and contributes positively to society.
  • Collaborate on addressing serious public concerns related to software.
  • Ensure honesty and fairness in all public communications.

Principle 2: Client and Employer

Software engineers shall act in a manner that is in the best interests of their client and employer, consistent with the public interest.

  • Operate within areas of expertise, being transparent about limitations.
  • Do not use software that is obtained or retained illegally or unethically.
  • Maintain confidentiality of professional information, unless law or public interest dictates otherwise.
  • Promptly report potential project failures or legal and cost issues to the employer or client.
  • Report significant social concerns related to software to the relevant authorities.
  • Place employer’s or client’s interests first unless ethical issues necessitate otherwise.

Principle 3: Product

Software engineers shall ensure that their products and related modifications meet the highest professional standards possible.

  • Aim for high quality, cost-efficiency, and timely delivery in projects, making trade-offs clear to all stakeholders.
  • Set achievable goals and ensure qualifications for projects through appropriate education, training, and experience.
  • Adhere to professional standards, using appropriate methods and documenting all specifications and solutions.
  • Provide realistic project estimates, perform thorough testing, and ensure comprehensive documentation.
  • Develop software that respects privacy, uses data ethically, and maintains data integrity.

Principle 4: Judgment

Software engineers shall maintain integrity and independence in their professional judgment.

  • Base technical decisions on the need to uphold human values.
  • Only endorse documents that are within their expertise and agreeable to them.
  • Maintain objectivity when evaluating software or related documents.
  • Avoid engaging in deceptive financial practices like bribery or double billing.
  • Disclose any conflicts of interest that are unavoidable to all relevant parties.

Principle 5: Management

Software engineering managers and leaders shall subscribe to and promote an ethical approach to the management of software development and maintenance.

  • Ensure effective procedures to promote quality and reduce risks.
  • Ensure that software engineers are informed of standards before being held to them.
  • Assign tasks based on education and experience, with opportunities for further development.
  • Provide realistic project estimates with an assessment of uncertainties.
  • Avoid preventing qualified individuals from obtaining positions they are suited for.
  • Ensure fair agreements on the ownership of intellectual property created by software engineers.

Principle 6: Profession

Software engineers shall advance the integrity and reputation of the profession consistent with the public interest.

  • Foster an organizational environment conducive to ethical behavior.
  • Contribute to the advancement of software engineering knowledge through participation in professional bodies and publications.
  • Avoid prioritizing personal interests over professional responsibilities.
  • Represent software characteristics accurately, avoiding misleading or speculative claims.
  • Take responsibility for detecting, correcting, and reporting errors.

Principle 7: Colleagues

Software engineers shall be fair to and supportive of their colleagues.

  • Promote adherence to this ethical code among colleagues.
  • Support the professional development of colleagues.
  • Properly acknowledge the contributions of others and avoid claiming undue credit.
  • Review others’ work objectively, candidly, and properly- documented way.
  • Seek input from professionals with the necessary relevant expertise (competence).

Principle 8: Self

Software engineers shall participate in lifelong learning regarding the practice of their profession and shall promote an ethical approach to the practice of the profession.

  • Continuously enhance knowledge and skills in all aspects of software development and management.
  • Aim to develop software that is safe, reliable, and cost-effective within set timelines.
  • Work on improving the quality, accuracy, and clarity of technical documentation.
  • Deepen understanding of the software and its operational environment.
  • Improve their knowledge of relevant standards and governing laws.
  • Do not give unfair treatment to anyone because of any irrelevant prejudices

Law in Technology

Warning

Warning

This is not legal advice. This is intended to highlight the complexity of the legal environment in which you may have to navigate.

Complexity of Law

  • Legal definitions are often ambiguous, and their implications can be inconsistent.
  • Laws are interpreted by humans, not machines, leading to variations in enforcement and application.
  • Laws vary by jurisdiction and change over time, and their interpretation can differ significantly between judges.

Legal penalties can be severe, ranging from multi-million dollar fines to jail time, and the destruction of inventory.

Types of Law

  • Statutes: Laws passed by both the U.S. Congress and state legislatures.
  • Regulations: Rules issued by government agencies to guide the enforcement of statutes.
  • Precedents: Judicial decisions from courts that establish guidelines for similar cases in the future.

Legal Systems

  • Federal and State Laws: The U.S. operates under a dual system of federal and state laws, each with its own courts.
  • International law is complex and especially regarding internet services.

Note

A user, the server they access, the server where their data is stored, and the headquarters of the company operating the service may all be located in different countries. Each country may claim jurisdiction over the service.

Key Computing-Specific Laws

  • Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA, 1986)
    • Prohibits unauthorized access to computing systems, aiming to protect the integrity of computer data and prevent fraud.
  • Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA, 1998)
    • Restricts the circumvention of access controls to copyrighted works.
    • Reduces the liability of service providers for infringing activities conducted by their users.

Key Computing-Specific Laws (cont.)

  • General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR, 2016) & California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA, 2020)
    • Imposes strict regulations on the use of personal data, requiring either user consent or a legitimate interest for data processing.
    • Recognizes browser cookies as personal data, subject to privacy standards.

Other Laws

  • Copyright Law: Protects original works of authorship, including software, from unauthorized copying.
  • Patent Law: Grants inventors exclusive rights to their inventions, including software algorithms.
  • Trademark Law: Protects brand names, logos, and slogans from unauthorized use.
  • Trade Secret Law: Protects confidential business information, including software source code.
  • Export Control Laws: Regulate the export of software and technology to foreign countries.
  • Antitrust Law: Prohibits monopolistic practices that restrict competition in the software industry.
  • Contract Law: Governs the formation and enforcement of software licenses and service agreements.
