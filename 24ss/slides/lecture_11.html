System Modeling

What is System Modeling?

  • System modeling is the process of developing abstract models of a system, with each model presenting a different view or perspective of that system.
  • Modeling employs diagrams or other graphical representations to effectively convey the architecture, functionality, and behavior of the system.
  • Models are highly beneficial for understanding and communicating the system’s design and for documenting the design for future reference.
  • In software engineering system modeling is the process of developing abstract models of a system, with each model presenting a different view or perspective of that system.
  • The model is often a simplified representation of the system, which captures the essential behavior of the system.

Leveraging Models in Requirements Engineering

  • In the requirements engineering phase, models of the existing (legacy) system are used to explain its functionalities. These models serve as a foundation for identifying the system’s strengths and weaknesses, which, in turn, inform the requirements for the new (modern) system.
  • For the new system, models are utilized within the requirements engineering process to clarify the proposed requirements to stakeholders. Engineers leverage these models to discuss design proposals and document the system in preparation for implementation.

Perspectives in System Modeling

A system can be viewed from various perspectives, each represented by different types of models. The four primary perspectives are:

  1. External Perspective: Looks at the system’s surroundings to understand its role and how it interacts with external factors and other systems. It’s about defining where the system begins and ends and seeing what outside influences affect it.

  2. Interaction Perspective: Focuses on how the system interacts with its environment and its own parts. This view is about tracing communication and data flow to see how different parts work together or with external elements.

Each of these perspectives offers valuable insights into different facets of the system, enabling a comprehensive analysis and understanding of its complexity, functionality, and interactions with its environment.

Perspectives in System Modeling (Cont.)

  1. Structural Perspective: Examines how the system is put together, including how its parts are organized and how data is managed. This perspective helps in understanding the system’s architecture and the connections between its components.

  2. Behavioral Perspective: Looks at what the system does in response to various situations, tracking processes, changes, and actions over time. It’s key for seeing how the system functions and adjusts to changes, both from within and outside.

Graphical Representations

Graphical representations play a vital role in the context of system analysis and design for several reasons:

  1. Facilitating Discussion: They are crucial in promoting discussions about either an existing or a proposed system. It is important to recognize that models may be incomplete or contain inaccuracies; their main purpose is to support discussion and conceptual understanding.

  2. Documenting Systems: When employed for documentation, models should accurately depict the system. While they do not need to detail every aspect of the system, the focus should be on providing a clear and precise overview rather than exhaustive detail.

Graphical Representations (Cont.)

  1. Detailed System Descriptions for Implementation: In situations where models serve as the foundation for system implementation, accuracy and completeness are imperative. These models are essential, guiding the development process to ensure that the final system conforms to the specified requirements and design.

Unified Modeling Language (UML)

Unified Modeling Language (UML)

  • The Unified Modeling Language (UML) is a standardized, general-purpose modeling language.
  • UML is not a programming language; rather, it is a visual language.
  • The original motivation for creating UML was to standardize the diverse notational systems and approaches used in software design.
  • UML 2.5, released in 2015, is the latest version of the language.
  • It was developed by Grady Booch, Ivar Jacobson, and James Rumbaugh at Rational Software in 1994–1995, with further development led by them through 1996.
  • UML is managed by the Object Management Group (OMG), an industry standards consortium.

Primary Goals of UML

  • Provide users with an expressive, ready-to-use visual modeling language to develop and exchange meaningful models.
  • Offer extensibility and specialization mechanisms for extending core concepts.
  • Maintain independence from specific programming languages and development processes.

Primary Goals of UML (cont.)

  • Establish a formal foundation for understanding the modeling language.
  • Foster the growth of the object-oriented tools market.
  • Facilitate support for higher-level development concepts, including collaborations, frameworks, patterns, and components.
  • Incorporate and integrate best practices.

As summarized by the Object Management Group (OMG).

UML Diagram Views

UML diagrams are categorized into two distinct views:

  • Structural (or Static) View: This view highlights the system’s static structure through objects, attributes, operations, and relationships. It encompasses class diagrams and composite structure diagrams.
  • Behavioral (or Dynamic) View: This view focuses on the system’s dynamic behavior by illustrating collaborations among objects and changes in the objects’ internal states. Included in this view are sequence diagrams, activity diagrams, and state machine diagrams.

The views in UML diagrams serve as tools to visually represent the concepts of the perspectives discussed earlier in system modeling.

UML: Types Diagrams

Structure Diagrams

Structure diagrams illustrate the static structure of the system being modeled. They model the static aspects of a system and generalize its main elements, such as classes, objects, interfaces, components, and nodes. These diagrams are used to depict the relationships among these elements and to highlight their properties.

Overview of Structure Diagrams

  • Class Diagram: This diagram shows the system’s classes, their attributes, operations, and the relationships among the classes.
  • Package Diagram: This diagram illustrates the system’s groupings and their dependencies.
  • Object Diagram: This provides a partial or complete view of a system’s structure at a specific time.

Overview of Structure Diagrams (contd.)

  • Composite Structure Diagram: This diagram details the internal structure of a classifier and the collaborations that this structure enables.
  • Component Diagram: This shows the components of a system and their interdependencies.
  • Profile Diagram: This metamodel depicts stereotypes as classes and profiles as packages.
  • Deployment Diagram: This diagram displays the hardware used in a system’s implementation, along with their deployment environments and artifacts.
  • The importance of different types of UML diagrams can vary depending on the phase of the project or the specific needs of the project.
  • Class Diagrams, Component Diagrams, and Deployment Diagrams are often considered the most crucial across a wide range of projects. They provide essential views of a system’s architecture, from high-level component organization to the detailed design of the classes that make up the system, and the physical deployment of these components on hardware infrastructure.

Behavior Diagrams

Behavior diagrams describe the dynamic aspects of a system. They illustrate how the system behaves and interacts with itself and other systems.

Overview of Behavior Diagrams

  • Activity Diagram: This diagram illustrates the workflows of components within a system, showing the flow from one activity to another.
  • Use Case Diagram: This diagram describes the interactions between the system and its users, detailing the actors, goals, and dependencies within the system.
  • State Machine Diagram: This diagram depicts the states of the system and the transitions between those states.

Interaction Diagrams

Interaction diagrams are utilized to visualize the flow of messages between objects within a system. They serve to model the dynamic aspects of the system. Interaction diagrams fall under the category of behavioral diagrams.

Overview of Interaction Diagrams

  • Sequence Diagrams: This represents object communication and lifespans through messages exchanged between them.
  • Communication Diagrams: This highlights object interactions through sequenced messages.
  • Timing Diagrams: This diagram illustrates timing constraints within a system.
  • Interaction Overview Diagrams: This provides a system overview, where each node represents a communication diagram.

Reltationships

A class may be involved in one or more relationships with other classes. A relationship can be one of the following types: association, aggregation, composition, inheritance, realization, and dependency.

The relationships between classes are shown as lines connecting the classes. The lines often have a label that describes the relationship.

Reltationships: Inheritance (or Generalization)

Represents an “is-a” relationship.

  • A solid line with a hollow arrowhead that point from the child to the parent class
  • The child class inherits the attributes and operations of the parent class

  • abstract class names are italicized to indicate that they are abstract classes.

Reltationships: (Simple) Association

  • Represents a structural link between two peer classes.
  • There is an association between Class1 and Class2
  • A solid line connecting two classes

Reltationships: Aggregation

A special type of association. It represents a “part of” relationship.

  • Class2 is part of Class1.
  • Many instances (denoted by the *) of Class2 can be associated with Class1.
  • A solid line with an unfilled diamond at the association end connected to the class of composite

Reltationships: Composition

A special type of aggregation where parts are destroyed when the whole is destroyed.

  • Objects of Class2 live and die with Class1.
  • Class2 cannot stand by itself.
  • A solid line with a filled diamond at the association connected to the class of composite

Reltationships: Dependency

Exists between two classes if the changes to the definition of one may cause changes to the other (but not the other way around).

  • Class1 depends on Class2
  • A dashed line with an open arrow

Relationship: Names

Good relation names make sense when you read them out loud:

  • “Every spreadsheet contains some number of cells”
  • “An expression evaluates to a value”

They often have a small arrowhead to show the direction in which direction to read the relationship, e.g., expressions evaluate to values, but values do not evaluate to expressions.

UML: Sequence Diagrams

Sequence Diagram

UML sequence diagrams model the flow of logic within your system in a visual manner, enabling you both to document and validate your logic, and are commonly used for both analysis and design purposes.

Elements of a Sequence Diagram

A sequence diagram consists of the following elements:

  • Lifelines: Represent different objects or components in the system.
  • Gates: Mark the start of a sequence diagram.
  • Activation Bars: Show when an object is active, typically when a method is being executed.
  • Messages: Represent communication between objects, with solid arrows for synchronous calls and dashed arrows for return messages or asynchronous calls.
  • Combined Fragments: Represent conditional or looping logic.
  • Optional (opt): Denotes an optional behavior.
  • Interaction Use: A reference to another sequence diagram or a part of one.
  • Reply Message: A return message from a call.
  • Synchronous Messages: Represent the interaction between objects that wait for a response before continuing.
  • Asynchronous Messages: Represent messages where the sender does not wait for an immediate response.
  • Message to Self: Indicates a self-call within the object.
  • Annotations: Provide additional information, such as «ajax», indicating an Ajax call, or «json», indicating the use of JSON data format.

UML: Profile Diagrams

A profile diagram is a type of structural diagram in the Unified Modeling Language (UML) that serves as a generic extension mechanism for customizing UML models to specific domains or platforms. These extension mechanisms refine the standard semantics in a strictly additive manner, ensuring no contradictions arise with the standard semantics. Profiles are constructed using stereotypes, tagged value definitions, and constraints, which are then applied to particular model elements such as Classes, Attributes, Operations, and Activities. A Profile comprises a set of such extensions that, when combined, tailor UML to a specific domain (such as aerospace, healthcare, or finance) or a particular platform (like J2EE or .NET).

Model Driven Engineering (MDE)

What is Model-Driven Engineering (MDE)

Model-Driven Engineering (MDE) is a software development approach that prioritizes the use of models in the design and construction of software systems. The core premise of MDE is that the software development process can be streamlined and automated by employing models to delineate the system and its components, subsequently generating code from these models.

MDE Principles

  • Abstraction: MDE leverages models to abstract the system and its components, allowing developers to concentrate on the system’s high-level design rather than the minutiae of implementation.
  • Automation: By generating code from models, MDE automates the software development cycle, which minimizes the necessity for manual coding and decreases the potential for human error.
  • Reusability: MDE encourages the reuse of existing models and components, enabling developers to utilize and extend preexisting designs and codebases instead of beginning anew.
  • Separation of Concerns: MDE compartmentalizes different facets of the system into distinct models, simplifying the focus on individual aspects and managing the system’s overall complexity more effectively.

MDE Process

  1. Modeling: The initiation of MDE involves constructing models that encapsulate the system and its components, employing various modeling languages and tools such as UML (Unified Modeling Language), SysML (Systems Modeling Language), or Domain-Specific Languages (DSLs).
  2. Transformation: Subsequent to model creation, model transformation tools are used to convert models into executable code. These tools process the models and output code that is ready for compilation.
  3. Code Generation: The code derived from the models is compiled to produce the operational software system.
  4. Validation and Verification: The concluding phase in MDE entails the validation and verification of the software to confirm its adherence to the required specifications and performance criteria.

MDE Tools

  • Modeling Languages: Tools such as UML, SysML, and various DSLs are employed for model creation.
  • Model Transformation Tools: ATL (Atlas Transformation Language), QVT (Query/View/Transformation), and Xtend are examples of transformation tools.
  • Code Generation Tools: Solutions like Acceleo, Xtend, and JET (Java Emitter Templates) facilitate the generation of code from models.
  • Model Validation and Verification Tools: Tools such as OCL (Object Constraint Language) and the EMF (Eclipse Modeling Framework) Validation Framework support the validation and verification of models.
